1 Data

1.1 Sample data

The data for this study was obtained by collecting information from all Sociology and Political Science scholars at all Dutch institutions. During the initial phase of data collection, all important information regarding the author’s position, university affiliation, names, IDs, departments, and more was gathered. The coordinator of this course, Jochem Tolsma, has provided a dataset with said information to be used in this project (source sunbelt? ). OpenAlex was used to scrape all published works, including important work-related information such as work id, concepts, citations, abstracts, and subjects, up until 2024 (Priem, Piwowar, and Orr (2022)). From this data, I created a final dataset (n=101) that included all authors from the Sociology and Political Science departments at Radboud University within the time frame of [INSERT TIME FRAME].

1.2 Dependent variable

The dependent variable in this dataset, indicated by the ties between scholars, refers to academic collaborations between them and are often called co-authorships. The data was scraped from OpenAlex API using the rvest and xml2 packages in R ( Priem, Piwowar, and Orr (2022), Wickham, Hester, and Ooms (2023), Wickham (2024)). The dependent variable, represented by an adjacency matrix, shows whether two actors or writers collaborated on one of their publications in the specified wave.
Each dyad (author combination) is assessed based on whether they collaborated (1) or not (0) throughout the data period.

  • In this study, ties are described as directional [ARE THEY??] which implies that they are delivered by one actor and received by another. This means each actor has both outdegrees and indegrees. Outdegrees refer to ‘sent’ connections that refer to being the first author of a manuscript. Indegrees, or ‘received’ connections, imply collaboration on an article without being the ‘initial’ author. The generated adjacency matrix can take the form of a network, indicating the collaboration patterns of the academics at Radboud University between YEAR AND YEAR.

1.3 Independent variables

1.3.1 Gender

The first covariate of my study is the gender of the scholars in the sample. This information was not available through OpenAlex, and was instead manually generated by consulting the website of the Dutch ‘Voornamenbank’ of the Meertens Institute (Instituut (n.d.)). If the website indicated that a name was used my more males than females, the scholar with that name was considered be male, and vice versa (female/male: 0/1).

For simplicity’s sake the concept of gender is not only inferred by the scholars name, but also assumed to be binary and indistinguishable from biological sex. I am aware that names do not equate to someone’s gender identity and that gender identities encompass more than men and women. Further I am also aware that the terms men and women refer to gender identities and the terms male and female usually refer to biological sex. In this study these words have been conflated and it should be noted that this is not representative of the current scientific understanding of biological sex and gender, and that the way of obtaining this variable disregards scholars’ self-identification of gender completely. This is done merely due to this project being part of a university assignment and is not something I would do, if I were to research outside of the context of my university curriculum.

1.3.2 Prestige

The scholars’ h-indices will serve as a control variable in this research design (find source : Bornmann et al. 2020). The h-index calculates the number of publications that have been cited at least as frequently as the author’s total number of publications. This number can then be used as measure for the author’s ‘prestige’ (Hirsch (2005)). The number of citations and publications needed for this calculation were webscraped through OpenAlex API (Priem, Piwowar, and Orr (2022)). The code for this process was provided by Lucan Bovens.

2 Methods

To evaluate the homophiliy in collaboration networks by gender and h-index, the analysis of the data will be as follows. The first step involves examining descriptives of the data to answer question of how homophily manifests in terms of degree and prestige within these networks. These descriptive findings will then serve as a foundation for the second phase of the analysis, which focuses on answering the explanatory research question of this study. This phase investigates the impact of homophily on collaboration and explores the interaction between gender and h-index. Descriptive analyses will utilize functions of the igraph and ggraph packages and t-tests, while the explanatory questions will be examined using Stochastic Actor-Based Models implemented via the RSiena package (Csárdi et al. (2024), Pedersen (2024), Snijders, Ripley, Boitmanis, et al. (2024), Snijders, Ripley, B’oda, et al. (2024)).

2.2 RSiena

SIENA, which stands for Simulation Investigation for Empirical Network Analysis, is a software tool designed to perform statistical estimation of models for longitudinal social network data. SIENA version 4.0, also known as RSiena, is a package for the R statistical software and was developed by Ruth Ripley and Krists Boitmanis, in collaboration with Tom Snijders (Snijders, Ripley, Boitmanis, et al. (2024), Snijders, Ripley, B’oda, et al. (2024)). The use of RSiena for this project is highly based on the book “Social Network Analysis for Social Scientists“ by Jochem Tolsma and Bas Hofstra (Tolsma and Hofstra (2022)).
