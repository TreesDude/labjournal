1.3 Independent variables

1.3.1 Gender

The first covariate of my study is the gender of the scholars in the sample. This information was not available through OpenAlex, and was instead manually generated by consulting the website of the Dutch ‘Voornamenbank’ of the Meertens Institute (Instituut (n.d.)). If the website indicated that a name was used my more males than females, the scholar with that name was considered be male, and vice versa (female/male: 0/1).

For the sake of simplicity, gender in this study was inferred from scholars’ names and treated as binary, conflating gender identity with biological sex. I acknowledge that names do not reliably indicate a person’s gender identity and that gender encompasses more than the categories of men and women. Additionally, I recognize that while men and women typically refer to gender identities, male and female are often used to denote biological sex. These distinctions were not upheld in this study, which conflates the terms and disregards individuals’ self-identified genders. This approach was taken solely due to the constraints of a university assignment and does not reflect my standard research practices, which would respect and account for the complexity of gender and self-identification outside the academic coursework context.

1.3.2 Prestige

The scholars’ h-indices will serve as a control variable in this research design (Bornmann, Ganser, Tekles, & Leydesdorff (2020), Bornmann, Ganser, & Tekles (2021)). The h-index calculates the number of publications that have been cited at least as frequently as the author’s total number of publications. This number can then be used as measure for the author’s ‘prestige’ (Hirsch (2005)). The number of citations and publications needed for this calculation were webscraped through OpenAlex API (Priem et al. (2022)). The code for this process was provided by Lucan Bovens.

2.2 RSiena

SIENA, which stands for Simulation Investigation for Empirical Network Analysis, is a software tool designed to perform statistical estimation of models for longitudinal social network data. SIENA version 4.0, also known as RSiena, is a package for the R statistical software and was developed by Ruth Ripley and Krists Boitmanis, in collaboration with Tom Snijders (Snijders, Ripley, Boitmanis, et al. (2024), Snijders, Ripley, B’oda, et al. (2024)). The use of RSiena for this project is highly based on the book “Social Network Analysis for Social Scientists“ by Jochem Tolsma and Bas Hofstra (Tolsma & Hofstra (2022)).

3 Ethical concerns

3.1 Webscraping

This study relies on data obtained through web scraping publicly available sources, which raises several ethical concerns. One key issue is that the scholars included in the dataset did not provide informed consent for the use of their data in this research. To address this, several measures were implemented. First, efforts were made to anonymize the data presented in this study to the greatest extent possible. Second, only information directly relevant to the research objectives was collected, minimizing potential intrusions on privacy. Third, the scraped data did not include sensitive or harmful content, thereby reducing the risk of any negative consequences arising from its use.

Despite these precautions, the fundamental issue of using non-consensual data persists. I do not adhere to the view that making data publicly available equates to granting unrestricted permission for its use in research. While the data in this study was already accessible online, the research generates new insights, such as patterns of tie formation between scholars, which were not previously explicit. Although names and other identifying details have been omitted to avoid recognizing individuals within network graphs or other outputs, informed consent for the use of personal data was not obtained. As such, this method of data collection remains ethically contentious at best.

3.2 Operationalization of gender

An additional ethical concern relates to the treatment of gender in this study. The method used to infer gender, based on names and a binary framework, may raise issues regarding inclusivity and respect for individuals’ self-identified gender. This approach disregards the complexity of gender identity and risks misrepresentation. A more detailed explanation of how gender data was defined and its limitations is provided in the data definition section.
