1 Data

1.1 Sample data

  • The data for this study was obtained by collecting information from all Sociology and Political Science scholars at all Dutch institutions. During the initial phase of data collection, all important information regarding the author’s position, university affiliation, names, IDs, departments, and more was gathered. The coordinator of this course, Jochem Tolsma, has provided a dataset with said information to be used in this project (source sunbelt? ). OpenAlex was used to scrape all published works, including important work-related information such as work id, concepts, citations, abstracts, and subjects, up until 2024 (Priem, Piwowar, and Orr (2022)). From this data, I created a final dataset (n=101) that included all authors from the Sociology and Political Science departments at Radboud University.

  • source open alex: Priem et al. (2022)

    • Priem, J., Piwowar, H., & Orr, R. (2022). OpenAlex: A fully-open index of scholarly works, authors, venues, institutions, and concepts. ArXiv. https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.01833

  • timeframe of focus

1.2 Dependent variable: Ties between scholars

  • The dependent variable in this dataset, indicated by the ties between scholars, refers to academic collaborations between them, often also known as co-authorships. The data was scraped from OpenAlex API using the rvest and XML packages in R ( Priem, Piwowar, and Orr (2022), Wickham, Hester, and Ooms (2023), Wickham (2024)). The dependent variable, represented by an adjacency matrix, shows whether two actors or writers collaborated on one of their publications in the specified wave.
    Each dyad (author combination) is assessed based on whether they collaborated (1) or not (0) throughout the data period, which in this case is the first or second wave.

  • In this study, ties are described as directional, which implies that they are delivered by one actor and received by another. This means each actor has both outdegrees and indegrees. Outdegrees refer to ‘sent’ connections that refer to being the first author of a manuscript. Indegrees, or ‘received’ connections, imply collaboration on an article without being the ‘initial’ author. The generated adjacency matrix can take the form of a network, indicating the collaboration patterns of the academics who worked at the aforementioned university between YEAR AND YEAR.

1.3 Independent variables

1.3.1 Gender

The first covariate of my study is the gender of the scholars in the sample. This information was not available through OpenAlex, and was instead manually generated by consulting the website of the Dutch ‘Voornamenbank’ of the Meertens Institute (Instituut (n.d.)). If the website indicated that a name was used my more males than females, the scholar with that name was considered be male, and vice versa (female/male: 0/1).

For simplicity’s sake the concept of gender is not only inferred by the scholars name, but also assumed to be binary and indistinguishable from biological sex. I am aware that names do not equate to someone’s gender identity and that gender identities encompass more than men and women. Further I am also aware that the terms men and women refer to gender identities and the terms male and female usually refer to biological sex. In this study these words have been conflated and it should be noted that this is not representative of the current scientific understanding of biological sex and gender, and that the way of obtaining this variable disregards scholars’ self-identification of gender completely. This is done merely due to this project being part of a university assignment and is not something I would do, if I were to research outside of the context of my university curriculum.

1.3.2 Prestige

The scholars’ h-indices will serve as a control variable in this research design (find source : Bornmann et al. 2020). The h-index calculates the number of publications that have been cited at least as frequently as the author’s total number of publications. This number can then be used as measure for the author’s ‘prestige’ (Hirsch (2005)). The number of citations and publications needed for this calculation were webscraped through OpenAlex API (Priem, Piwowar, and Orr (2022)). The code for this process was provided by a fellow course mate, namely Lucan Bovens.

  • definition

    • “SIENA, shorthand for Simulation Investigation for Empirical Network Analysis, is a computer program that carries out the statistical estimation of models for repeated measures of social networks according to the dynamic actor-oriented model of Snijders and van Duijn (1997), Snijders (2001), and Snijders, Steglich, and Schweinberger (2007); also see Steglich, Snijders, and Pearson (2009). A tutorial for these models is in Snijders, van de Bunt, and Steglich (2009). Some examples are presented, e.g., in van de Bunt (1999); van de Bunt, van Duijn, and Snijders (1999); and van Duijn, Zeggelink, Stokman, and Wasseur (2003); and Steglich, Snijders, and West (2006). A website for SIENA is maintained at http://www.stats.ox.ac.uk/\~snijders/siena/ . At this website (‘publications’ tab) you shall also find references to introductions in various other languages. This is a provisional manual for SIENA version 4.0, which is also called RSiena. This is a contributed package for the R statistical system which can be downloaded from http://cran.r-project.org. For the operation of R, the reader is referred to the corresponding manual. If desired, SIENA can be operated apparently independently of R, as is explained in Section 2.1. RSiena was programmed by Ruth Ripley and Krists Boitmanis, in collaboration with Tom Snijders.”
